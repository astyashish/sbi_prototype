Here are five examples of promising AI research in quantum computing:

* **Quantum Machine Learning**: Quantum machine learning is a subfield of quantum computing that focuses on developing machine learning algorithms that can be run on quantum computers. This field has the potential to revolutionize the way we approach machine learning, enabling faster and more efficient processing of complex data sets. Quantum machine learning algorithms, such as quantum k-means and quantum support vector machines, have been shown to outperform their classical counterparts in certain tasks.

Background: Quantum machine learning is based on the principles of quantum mechanics, which allow for the processing of vast amounts of data in parallel. This is achieved through the use of quantum bits, or qubits, which can exist in multiple states simultaneously.

Why it's useful: Quantum machine learning has the potential to solve complex problems in fields such as image recognition, natural language processing, and predictive analytics. It could also enable the development of more accurate and efficient machine learning models, leading to breakthroughs in areas such as healthcare and finance.

* **Quantum Simulation**: Quantum simulation is a technique that uses quantum computers to simulate the behavior of complex quantum systems. This field has the potential to revolutionize our understanding of quantum mechanics and enable the development of new materials and technologies.

Background: Quantum simulation is based on the principles of quantum mechanics, which allow for the simulation of complex quantum systems using quantum computers. This is achieved through the use of quantum algorithms, such as the quantum circuit model and the adiabatic algorithm.

Why it's useful: Quantum simulation has the potential to enable the development of new materials and technologies, such as more efficient solar cells and more powerful batteries. It could also enable the simulation of complex quantum systems, leading to breakthroughs in our understanding of quantum mechanics.
